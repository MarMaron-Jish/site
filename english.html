St. Maron Church

Since the Church of the Virgin Mary was no more than 100 square meters, we began to think about building a new church bearing the name of the patron saint of the Maronite Church, Saint Maron. Work began in 1980, and on February 21, 1982, the late Bishop Youssef Khoury laid the foundation stone; then, his successor, the late Bishop Maron Sader, inaugurated the church on August 24, 1996, amidst a majestic crowd of bishops and priests of the Catholic Church in the Holy Land and of the sons and daughters of the parishes of Galilee.


Four electric bells from Greece, 2009
With Abbot Augustine Harfoush and friend Ibrahim Issa near the foundation stone

The work did not end with the inauguration of the church, but continued to this day. The church was equipped with new seats and Czech crystal chandeliers, and in 2004, with the Vitrage, which decorated 60 windows with Maronite icons representing major feasts, famous saints, and the mysteries of the Rosary. In 2005, the church was inlaid from the outside with stone, and until that year the costs of building the church and halls amounted to more than 3.5 million dollars, the vast majority of which came from donations of money or labor from parishioners, and the rest from external donations. In 2008-2009, a bell tower about 25 meters high was built, and in 2017, a wooden entrance was built. In 2018, a large-scale restoration of the church was carried out from the inside, which included the addition of a huge central air conditioning unit, decorating the ceiling, the structure, and the church columns with wood, and inlaying the two walls adjacent to the structure with shekel stone.


The church covers an area of ​​about 600 m2, while the halls on the lower floor cover an area of ​​about 1,000 m2. These halls include the youth club and the library (about 200 m2, restored in 2019; the library contains dozens of encyclopedias, dictionaries and hundreds of books), the large hall (about 350 m2), the diwan (120 m2, part of which was restored in 2020), the church lounge (70 m2), the shelter (60 m2) and some rooms. There are also three courtyards surrounding the church from the north, east and south with an area of ​​more than 3,500 m2. All of these places serve the parish in its joys, sorrows and various activities.

St. Maron's movements and activities





The Pastoral Council: It includes a number of the best men in the parish; they accompany the parish priests in visiting the homes of the faithful, in giving communion to the elderly, and in visiting the sick in the hospital, and they set a good example through their religious practice and personal sacrifice.




Endowment Committee: Although it has been limited in the last ten years to only two members, it has not failed to ensure the interest of the endowment, invest in it, and participate in making decisions to establish projects and restore and maintain endowment buildings. I must stop at its two members:


• Committee member, Brother Benoit Habib: He helps me with reviewing accounts, preparing the annual budget, correspondence, communications, and all necessary publications. He has an office for various insurances in the Waqf building. He is by my side at all times and is ready to leave his work and provide any emergency assistance. Because of his knowledge, morals, and honesty, I have made him a personal friend and secretary for twenty years. I cannot thank him enough for his sacrifices and donations to the church, but I ask God to protect him and all members of his family.

• Committee member, Brother Deeb Emtans Maroun: He specializes in the field of construction. He is my personal advisor in urban affairs due to his expertise in engineering, design, and project implementation. He does not skimp on me or the church in any way. This is not strange, as his father, the late Emtans Abdullah Maroun, was one of the closest people to me and my work in the church of Jish and Kafr Bir’im. He always accompanied me in preparing the work assessments and preparing the budgets required for each stage of construction. He and his partners, Mr. Ibrahim Issa and the late Nimer Shukri Yaqoub, completed the first stage of construction on the ground floor, and they continued to provide assistance in the subsequent stages.

Church Choir: It serves the Mass and religious celebrations, and with its beautiful voices it transports the faithful from the earthly to the heavenly. It also takes care of cleaning the church and decorating it on major occasions.



Immaculate Conception Brotherhood: meets once a week to pray and meditate on the Holy Bible and the Maronite Liturgy; they also visit the elderly and help clean the church; after Sunday Mass, they prepare coffee and refreshments for the parishioners in the church hall and for guests from outside the village.



Concert: In recent years, several concerts (chanting and classical religious music) have been held in the Church of St. Maron, most of them directed by Mrs. "Ada Belge" and with the participation of dozens of the best musicians and singers and between 500-800 people from all over the country. These gatherings have contributed to revitalizing the economy in the village, such as restaurants and shops, and to putting the village of Jish on the country's tourist map. Part of the costs were contributed by the billionaire "Steve Wardheimer"; this great and decent man established a factory near the village that employs a large number of our children. One day, he invited me to his office to participate in breakfast and talk about the economic situation of the village. I arrived at the appointed time to his office, and his business manager received me and called him saying: "His Holiness the Father has arrived." But he had forgotten the appointment and went to inspect one of his factories in the Nahariya area, so he replied: "I will be in five minutes." Moments later, his small plane landed next to the office and he entered and apologized for being late. This 95 year old man has made a huge impression on me.



Religious trips: The parish used to organize annual trips to the main shrines in Europe, especially Lourdes and Fatima; it also organized annual trips to the holy places in Jerusalem, Bethlehem, Nazareth and Tiberias. These trips included visiting Italy, France and Spain six times; visiting Spain and Portugal four times; and a trip to Greece (Athens and some islands). A trip to Northern Europe: Paris, Brussels, Cologne, Amsterdam, The Hague, Reims, Amiens, Rouen, Lisieux, Versailles, Chartres and London. A trip to Russia: Moscow and Petersburg. A trip to the Scandinavian countries: Sweden, Denmark, Finland, Norway. A trip to Egypt: Cairo and Mount Saint Catherine with the catechumens. Two trips to Jordan to meet His Beatitude Patriarch Sfeir and inaugurate the Church of Saint Charbel in Amman. Two trips to Lebanon with the dear Deacon Sobhi Makhoul (thanks for his efforts), to visit Annaya, Harissa, the Valley of the Saints and other holy places. More than 700 people participated in these trips (about 20 in number). The most important trips were in 1998 on the occasion of the beatification of the Venerable Nehmatallah Hardini, in 2001 on the occasion of the canonization of Sister Rafqa, and in 2004 the canonization of Blessed Nehmatallah Hardini.

Mercy Fund: Since the 1980s, the parish has been providing food and medicine to our brothers in southern Lebanon. In 2000, it provided about 21 tons of food to our Palestinian brothers in Jerusalem, some of which were contributed by brothers from the Islamic community. In 2021, the parish, at the initiative of the Apostolic Movement, provided food and cleaning supplies to orphans in Bethlehem, weighing more than five tons. Over the past few years, the parish has donated about $30,000 to the needy in Syria and Lebanon, through several venerable bishops. In 2020 and 2021 (during the Corona pandemic), the parish donated $150,000 to our people in Kafr Bir’im and Jish in Lebanon. The parish has also not been short in helping the needy locally. And this is just the tip of the iceberg.

The Convent of the Nuns: In 1956, with the help of the late Monsignor Fergani, the convent was built on the land of the Waqf and the Rosary Sisters lived there, serving the parish until 1997. After that, the Maronite Sisters of Saint Therese of the Child Jesus came to serve the parish. In 1991, more than 80 m2 were added to the first floor of the convent, and in 1998, another 80 m2 were added to the second floor, and the entire convent was furnished with new furniture after a radical renovation of the old building. In 2018, we carried out additional renovations inside the convent, and inlaid the northern and western facades with stone. The Rosary Sisters served the parish for 40 years, as did the Sisters of Saint Therese of the Child Jesus for 20 years, and we will not forget these nuns who worked in the parish in our prayers.

Church of Our Lady

The Church of Our Lady stands as a witness to the Maronite presence in Jish for decades. We do not know the year the church was built, but we do know that it was rebuilt in 1838, a year after the earthquake that destroyed the village. There is a stone in the church that dates this event, and it is engraved on it:


The construction of this shrine was renewed after the devastation and collapse
of an earthquake that disturbed all creatures and people,
with the presence of...our honorable shepherd, Bishop Abdullah, the respected,
at the hands of the most insignificant of all, Father Stephan. He hopes to conclude
this matter. They dated the end of the pain of grief in the year 1838.


The church has been restored several times, the last restoration being in 2021.

St. Peter and Paul Church

Since I took over the parish service, I knew that my responsibility was not only towards my parishioners, but extended beyond that to include the Greek Catholic parishioners of St. Peter and Paul, especially between the years 1975 and 2000, when I was the only priest in the village, and I was assigned to serve the sister parish, and I never differentiated between Peter and Maron, for we are all for Christ. I bear witness here that our Greek Catholic brothers contributed, like their Maronite brothers, to the construction of St. Maron Church, and donated money and labor. I cannot forget in particular the late Nakhl Zakaria Haddad and his brother Adeeb Haddad, and the brilliant engineer George Youssef Haddad.

A historical village, known in ancient times as "Jiskala." From it came Yohannan the Jeshi, the leader of the Jewish revolt against the Romans, and from it came the family of Saint Paul before leaving for Tarsus. In his book Famous Men, Saint Jerome (+420), one of the Church Fathers, wrote the following:


“Paul, who was formerly called Saul, was an apostle outside the Twelve, from the tribe of Benjamin and the city of Giscala in Judea. When Giscala was occupied by the Romans, Paul left with his parents for Tarsus in Cilicia. His parents sent him to Jerusalem to study the law, and he was educated by Gamaliel, a sign mentioned by Luke. But after witnessing the martyrdom of Stephen and receiving letters from the high priest of the temple to persecute those who believed in Christ, he traveled to Damascus. Being bound to the faith by revelation, as is written in the Acts of the Apostles, he was transformed from a persecutor into a chosen vessel.”


Some Bible scholars mention that Jesus Christ entered the village on his way from Tiberias to the Tyre and Sidon region via Wadi al-Amud.


Jish is located in the Upper Galilee at an altitude of 835 meters. According to state statistics for 2019, the village is home to 3,105 people, 1,524 males and 1,580 females. About 37% (1,148) of them are Muslims, 11% (341) are Greek Catholics, and 52% (1,614) are Maronites. It has an old mosque and three churches: the Greek Catholic Church of St. Peter and Paul (built in 1906), the Maronite Church of Our Lady (rebuilt in 1838), and the Maronite Church (inaugurated on 24.8. 1996).


The area of ​​Jish lands is about 12,500 dunums, of which about 2,500 dunums are under irrigation and require 935,000 cubic meters of water. It is worth mentioning that agriculture in Jish is advanced, and the fruits are delicious due to the fertile land and wonderful weather.


There are two schools in the village, an elementary school with about 373 students from grades one to eight and a high school affiliated with the Roman Catholic Church with about 177 students. The village is distinguished by the high cultural level of its male and female students. There are dozens who hold a first degree, a good number who hold a second degree and others who hold a third degree. According to state statistics for 2019, 40.7% of the village’s population, aged 25-54, hold a university degree. Also, according to state statistics, the average monthly income per capita is $1,341.


The village has a post office, a Leumi Bank branch, a Clalit health fund and a Leumit health fund, a Histadrut home, a nursing home, a community center, a soccer field, and several basketball courts.

Kafr Bir'im

date


It is an ancient village located on the Lebanese border. Its inhabitants were displaced in 1948; some of them moved to Lebanon and others to Jish. The Supreme Court ruled in 1952 in favor of the people of Kafr Birem, but successive governments refused to implement the ruling, and one of them even gave the order to destroy the village in 1953. The population before the displacement was about 1,000 people (150 houses) and its land area was about 12,500 dunams, of which about 1,000 dunams were registered in the name of the Maronite Waqf.


Our Lady Church in Kafr Bir'im

There is no doubt that the history of this church dates back to the late eighteenth century. In 1860, the church was burned with its contents and records, and after more than ten years, the locals built the current church. In 1926, with the beginning of the use of cement in the area, the people of Bir'am poured the roof of the church with cement, and they had collected the necessary gravel from the village's graves. Since the displacement in 1948, the church was closed and no one was allowed to open it, but in 1972, the people of Bir'am went to the church and opened it, and hundreds of them staged a sit-in in their stricken village, and clashed with police and army forces, so the parish priest, Father Bishara Suleiman, and fourteen men were arrested and thrown in prison. A trial was held for them the next day, and they were acquitted. The locals restored the church, and during the following years, many baptisms and wreaths were held there, and mass was celebrated on Christmas, Palm Sunday, and Easter. Since 2009, the Divine Mass has been celebrated weekly in the church.

It is the beating heart of the parish. The apostolic movement emerged in the parish in the 1980s, but it took on a new look with the arrival of the Maronite Sisters of Saint Therese of the Child Jesus in late 1998. The movement consists of several groups: the Knights (students from kindergarten to sixth grade), the Marines (students in seventh and eighth grades), the Pioneers (students in ninth and tenth grades), the Youth (students in eleventh and twelfth grades), the Missionaries (university students), and the Married Group. The movement holds weekly spiritual-social meetings, in addition to several internal formations and trips throughout the year; in addition, the movement organized four trips for the Knights’ guides abroad: Britain in 2016, Malta in 2017, France in 2018, and Germany and Austria in 2019. The movement’s activities culminate in a summer camp that lasts about ten days on the church grounds; In 2019, the number of participants in the camp reached 318 boys and girls and about 50 male and female guides.
